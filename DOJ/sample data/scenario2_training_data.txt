DOJ Civil Rights Division - Special Litigation Section
Subject: Pattern or Practice Investigation - Riverside Police Department
Analysis Period: March 2028

CURRENT LEGAL ISSUES REQUIRING ANALYSIS:

1. EXCESSIVE FORCE PATTERNS
   - Evidence: 47 use-of-force incidents in 18-month period (Jan 2027-June 2028)
   - Timeline: 12 incidents involved firearms, 28 involved impact weapons, 7 involved K-9 deployments
   - Impact: 15 individuals hospitalized, 3 fatalities (two officer-involved shootings, one in-custody death)
   - Legal Issue: Fourth Amendment unreasonable seizure claims under Graham v. Connor
   - Conflicting factors: Officer safety vs. proportionality vs. de-escalation requirements

2. DISCRIMINATORY POLICING ALLEGATIONS
   - Issue: Traffic stops data showing disparate impact on Black and Latino motorists
   - Timeline: 2026-2028 data analysis (65% of stops Black/Latino residents in 28% minority city)
   - Impact: Consent decree requirement under 34 U.S.C. § 12601, community trust erosion
   - Legal Interest: Equal Protection Clause violations, Title VI compliance
   - Conflicting factors: Legitimate law enforcement vs. pretextual stops vs. community relations

3. CUSTODIAL INTERROGATION PRACTICES
   - Evidence: 23 interrogations lacking Miranda warnings or voluntary waiver
   - Timeline: County Public Defender complaints filed March 15, 2028
   - Impact: Potential suppression of confessions, § 1983 civil liability
   - Legal Issue: Fifth Amendment Miranda violations, Fourteenth Amendment due process
   - Conflicting factors: Effective investigation vs. constitutional safeguards vs. conviction integrity

4. ACCOUNTABILITY AND OVERSIGHT FAILURES
   - Issue: Internal Affairs investigations closed without discipline in 89% of complaints
   - Timeline: FY2026-2027 review showing 156 complaints, 17 sustained findings
   - Impact: Pattern of inadequate investigation under DOJ guidelines
   - Legal Interest: Consent decree monitoring, civilian oversight requirements
   - Conflicting factors: Police union agreements vs. public accountability vs. discipline standards

5. TRAINING AND SUPERVISION DEFICIENCIES
   - Issue: Use-of-force training conducted only biennially, no implicit bias curriculum
   - Timeline: Last department-wide training completed October 2026
   - Impact: Failure to establish constitutional policing standards per DOJ Technical Assistance Letter
   - Legal Interest: Deliberate indifference under Monell municipal liability
   - Conflicting factors: Budget constraints vs. training needs vs. constitutional compliance

6. MENTAL HEALTH CRISIS RESPONSE GAPS
   - Issue: 34 force incidents involved individuals experiencing mental health crises
   - Timeline: No Crisis Intervention Team (CIT) program despite 2025 recommendations
   - Impact: Americans with Disabilities Act compliance, preventable injuries/deaths
   - Legal Interest: Title II ADA violations, 42 U.S.C. § 12132
   - Conflicting factors: Officer training vs. mental health resources vs. community safety

LEGAL FRAMEWORK CONSIDERATIONS:

Pattern or Practice Authority (34 U.S.C. § 12601):
- Statutory basis for investigation and enforcement (primary relevance: all issues)
- Requirement to show systematic constitutional violations (high relevance: force, discrimination)
- Consent decree remedies under judicial supervision (high relevance: accountability, training)
- Technical assistance as alternative to litigation (moderate relevance: if cooperation exists)

DOJ Civil Rights Division Priorities (per Assistant Attorney General guidance):
1. Address excessive force and use-of-force policies (critical: force patterns, supervision)
2. Eliminate discriminatory policing practices (critical: traffic stops, equal protection)
3. Ensure constitutional conditions of confinement (moderate: custodial interrogations)
4. Protect rights of vulnerable populations (critical: mental health, ADA compliance)
5. Promote transparency and accountability (critical: internal affairs, civilian oversight)

ANALYTICAL CONFLICTS TO ADDRESS:

Constitutional Standards vs. Operational Reality:
- Force: Graham "objectively reasonable" standard requires immediate threat assessment but officers face split-second decisions
- Discrimination: Proving discriminatory intent (Arlington Heights) vs. showing disparate impact (statistical evidence)
- Miranda: Voluntary waiver requirements vs. practical interrogation techniques

Immediate Enforcement vs. Long-Term Reform:
- Consent decree litigation protects rights immediately but requires years of judicial supervision
- Technical assistance preserves cooperative relationship but lacks enforceability
- Individual prosecutions address specific misconduct but don't reform systemic practices

Federal vs. Local Authority Balance:
- DOJ pattern or practice authority vs. local police department autonomy
- Federal court supervision via consent decree vs. city/county governance
- Community input and local accountability vs. federally-imposed reforms
